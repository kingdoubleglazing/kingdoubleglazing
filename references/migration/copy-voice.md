# Copy and Voice Rules

**Purpose.** Every word on the King Double Glazing site must pass these rules. This document exists because the previous version of the site over-indexed on SEO keywords and technical accuracy at the cost of plain-language conversion. The client rejected it for being too complicated. These rules fix that.

**The test.** Could a tired 40-year-old Melbourne homeowner, scrolling on their phone after work, understand this in 5 seconds without looking anything up? If no, rewrite.

---

## 1. The three laws

**Law 1: Every benefit gets a number.**
No number = cut the line or find one. "Quieter windows" is dead. "Up to 70% quieter" is alive. This applies to headlines, subheadings, bullets, testimonials, and CTA copy.

**Law 2: Jargon is allowed only if translated and followed by a number.**
The client specifically asked for this: "we do acoustics which makes it this percentage quieter." That's the template. Every technical term must be introduced with a plain-English explanation and immediately given a measurable benefit.

- ✅ *"Acoustic glass — that's a special type of glass that blocks sound — makes rooms up to 70% quieter."*
- ✅ *"We add a heat-blocking coating (it's invisible but it cuts heat transfer by up to 50%)."*
- ❌ *"Low-E coated argon-filled IGUs provide superior thermal performance."*

**Law 3: 8th-grade reading level, always.**
- Sentences: 8–12 words. Maximum 15.
- No subordinate clauses. No semicolons.
- No three-syllable word where a one-syllable word will do.
- Use "we" and "you", not "the customer" or "homeowners".
- Active voice. Always.

---

## 2. Banned words (never in headlines, rarely in body)

These words are banned from H1s, H2s, hero subheads, and CTA buttons. They're allowed in body copy only if immediately translated per Law 2.

| Banned word | Use instead |
|---|---|
| Low-E | "heat-blocking coating" |
| Argon | "extra insulation gas between the panes" |
| IGU | "insulated glass unit" or just "double-glazed glass" |
| U-value | don't mention — use "% warmer in winter" |
| R-value | don't mention — use "% warmer in winter" |
| dB / decibel | don't mention — use "% quieter" |
| Thermal performance | "keeps heat in" or "keeps heat out" |
| Acoustic performance | "blocks noise" |
| Retrofit (in headlines) | "upgrade your existing windows" |
| Glazing (in headlines) | "windows" |
| VEU | DO NOT USE — compliance risk |
| Rebate / government rebate | DO NOT USE — compliance risk |
| Accredited | DO NOT USE — compliance risk |
| Cheapest | "we beat any quote by 30%" |
| Best in Melbourne | don't use — unprovable superlative |
| "Full replacement is bad" framing | "Full replacement is sometimes necessary" — neutral tone only |
| Number one | don't use — unprovable superlative |
| Industry-leading | don't use — meaningless |
| Premium | don't use — means nothing |
| Solutions | "windows" or "upgrades" or the actual thing |
| Leverage | "use" |
| Utilise | "use" |
| Commence | "start" |

**Retrofit and glazing** are allowed in body copy *once explained*. After the explanation, they can be used as normal words. The rule is: first mention = plain-English; second mention onward = OK.

## 4. Benefit statement formulas

Every benefit on the site should fit one of these patterns:

**Formula A: Outcome + % + comparison**
*"Rooms up to 50% warmer than with single-pane glass."*

**Formula B: Problem → Fix → Number**
*"Tram noise keeping you up? Our acoustic glass cuts it by up to 70%."*

**Formula C: Money saved**
*"Heating bills drop by up to 40% after upgrading."*

**Formula D: Time**
*"Installed in one day. Zero mess, zero frame damage."*

**Formula E: Comparison anchor**
*"Full window replacement: $15,000+. Retrofit with us: from $495/m². Same result."*

## 5. Headline rules

- H1s should be one idea, maximum 8 words.
- Subheads can be longer but must contain at least one number.
- No puns. No cleverness. No "Let's Talk Windows".
- Every H2 on the page should preview the section in plain English.

**Good H1 examples:**
- "Stop Overpaying for Double Glazing."
- "Quieter, Warmer Windows. Half the Price."
- "We'll Beat Any Quote by 30%."

**Bad H1 examples (these all exist in the current codebase — rewrite them):**
- "The Problem Is Clear" — vague, no number, no hook
- "Why Retrofit?" — a question, not a promise
- "Melbourne-Wide Coverage" — not a benefit
- "Comprehensive Glazing Solutions" — meaningless jargon

---

## 6. CTA button copy rules

Every primary CTA button across the site must:
1. Start with a verb
2. Use "My" or "Your" to make it personal
3. Be ≤ 6 words
4. Make the next action obvious

**Approved primary CTAs:**
- `Get My Instant Price →`
- `Start My Free Estimate →`
- `Send My Quote — Get 30% Off →`
- `Book My Free Home Visit →`

**Approved secondary CTAs:**
- `Or call 0406 470 595`
- `See How It Works ↓`
- `Get 30% Off My Quote →`

**Banned CTAs:**
- ❌ "Submit"
- ❌ "Click here"
- ❌ "Learn more" (too vague, no commitment)
- ❌ "Contact us" (no benefit, no urgency)
- ❌ "Sign up" (wrong register for a trades site)

## 7. Tone rules

- **Warm, not corporate.** You're a Melbourne glazier, not a marketing agency.
- **Direct, not salesy.** "We'll beat any quote by 30%" beats "Discover our unbeatable savings".
- **Confident, not pushy.** Lifetime warranty is a fact. "Act now!!!" is a tell.
- **Local.** "Melbourne" or a suburb name should appear on every page at least once.
- **Honest about trade-offs.** The comparison table shows Full Replacement has the same noise/warmth benefits — it's just slower and more expensive. Don't pretend retrofit is magic.

## 8. Specific phrases to use throughout

These are brand anchors. Use them verbatim, don't rephrase:

- **"Stop — Don't Overpay."** (core tagline)
- **"We'll beat any genuine quote by 30%."** (offer)
- **"Lifetime warranty on every job."** (trust)
- **"50+ years combined experience."** (credential — note "combined")
- **"Melbourne-owned."** (local)
- **"Half the price of full replacement."** (positioning)
- **"Installed in a day."** (speed)
- **"Same frames. Same look. A fraction of the cost."** (retrofit explanation)
- **"We do full replacement too — just ask."** (secondary service acknowledgement, used sparingly, only on the homepage comparison table and the /services page)

## 9. Word count targets per section (homepage)

Short is better. These are ceilings, not targets.

| Section | Word count ceiling |
|---|---|
| Hero H1 | 6 words |
| Hero subhead | 25 words |
| Problem block (3 lines) | 45 words total |
| Fix block (3 lines) | 45 words total |
| Comparison table | table only + 15-word intro |
| Why us cards | 15 words per card |
| Process steps | 12 words per step |
| Founder story | 80 words |
| Testimonials | 25 words each |
| FAQ answers | 35 words each |

**Why ceilings not minimums.** The previous version of the site padded copy to hit SEO word counts (2000+ words per page). Research 1 and 2 confirm this is a widely circulated myth — padding hurts conversion. Match competitors, not an arbitrary target. The new homepage should land between 800 and 1,200 words total. Any more and you're competing with the estimate tool for attention.

## 10. The 8th-grade check

Before finalising any paragraph, run it through this check:

1. **Sentence length:** Is any sentence longer than 15 words? Break it.
2. **Syllables:** Is there a three-syllable word where a one-syllable word works? Replace it.
3. **Jargon:** Did I use a technical term without translating it? Fix it.
4. **Number:** Did I claim a benefit without backing it with a number or comparison? Add one or cut it.
5. **Active voice:** Did I use passive voice ("is installed" vs "we install")? Rewrite it.
6. **"We" and "you":** Am I talking to the customer directly, or about them? Fix pronouns.
7. **Read-out-loud test:** Does it sound like how Tas would explain it to a neighbour at the pub? If no, rewrite.

If a paragraph passes all 7, ship it.

## 12. What to do when the research files say one thing and these rules say another

These rules win. The research files informed the funnel structure and the conversion tactics, but the voice and reading level are set by what the client asked for: "simple enough for an 8th grader to understand." If a research citation recommends a phrase like "thermal performance coefficient", ignore it and use "keeps heat in" with a percentage. The research is directional; these rules are absolute.
